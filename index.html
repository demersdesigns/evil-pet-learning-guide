Evil Pet Tutorial & Performance Tips — Course Plan

Fora beginner-to-novice granular user with a large, ambient/generative-leaning Eurorack rig

Goalget comfortable with the Evil Pet's controls fast, then integrate it as a "granular voice" inside an existing modular workflow

0. What the Evil Pet actually is (orientation, not a lesson)

  • Desktop/pedal-format 8-voice granular processor and synth from Endorphin.es, not a Eurorack module — it patches in via 1/4" jacks, MIDI/MPE, or its own mic/radio/SD card.
  • Four ways to get source audio in: line inputs, built-in mic, built-in FM radio tuner, or samples loaded from a microSD card. It records into a buffer (up to ~10 minutes mono, adjustable down to a few seconds).
  • Two basic modes of use: (1) a synth you play from a MIDI/MPE keyboard, or (2) a real-time effects processor you run other gear through — exactly like a granular pedal.
  • Front panel is "everything visible" — most granular parameters are direct knobs rather than buried in menus, with a shift key for secondary functions.

This matters for your rig specifically: it's an outboard voice/effect, so the tutorial plan below treats it as something you route your existing modules through, not a module you patch CV into directly (it has no CV ins — control comes from its own knobs, LFOs, envelope follower, or MIDI).

Part 1: Foundations (Sessions 1–3)

Session 1Get sound in, get sound out

  • Patch a single Eurorack voice (start with something simple and melodic, e.g. Airstreamer or a basic oscillator) into the Evil Pet's line input.
  • Leave all granular controls at defaults; just confirm dry signal passes through (Bypass button).
  • Try the built-in mic and the FM radio as sources too — no patching needed, good for understanding "any audio in becomes grain material."
  • Concept to land: grains are short slices of whatever's in the buffer, not a live pass-through. Recording vs. processing-in-real-time is the first fork in the road.

Session 2The four core knobs: Position / Size(Cloud) / Grains / Pitch

  • Position: where in the buffer playback happens.
  • Size/Cloud (shift function): how wide a window around Position the grains are drawn from.
  • Grains: density/rate of grain generation — 12 o'clock = one grain at a time, right = denser, left = more randomized/sparse triggering.
  • Pitch/Detune: transposition (quantized or free) and per-grain pitch randomization.
  • Exercise: freeze a single vowel sound or sustained pad from your rig and slowly sweep Position while keeping Size small — this is the clearest way to hear what a "grain" is before adding modulation.

Session 3Spread, Filter, and gain staging

  • Spread: stereo placement of grains.
  • Filter: cutoff/resonance on one knob, multiple modes including a DJ-style low-to-high-pass sweep — good first "performance" control because it's immediately musical.
  • Practical note from user reports: some units are prone to crackling/aliasing at extreme Grains/Size settings regardless of gain staging — set input trim conservatively and treat crackle as a texture option, not always a fault, once you've ruled out overs.

Part 2: Motion & Character (Sessions 4–6)

Session 4Modulation sources

  • Three LFOs (shape + speed) assignable by press-and-twist to any knob.
  • Envelope follower — takes shape from the incoming audio, so a rhythmic modular sequence can "drive" the Evil Pet without a shared clock.
  • ADSR — for when you're playing it as a synth from MIDI/MPE.
  • Exercise: route your Pamela's PRO Workout output into the Evil Pet's line/audio path as an audio-rate modulation source (not CV — remember, no CV ins), then use the envelope follower to let its rhythm control grain density.

Session 5The Saturator (it's really four effects in one)

  • Distortion, sample-rate reduction, bit-crushing, chorus/flanger, and — the standout — Feedback mode, where granulated output re-enters the buffer.
  • Feedback is the fastest route to self-sustaining ambient drones and can self-oscillate; several users note it's easy to get runaway feedback, so approach the knob slowly and know where Volume/Mute is.
  • Exercise: with nothing patched in, use only the built-in mic + Feedback mode to build a drone from room tone — a good "no-input" performance move for live sets.

Session 6Reverb and the Random button

  • Reverb modes (plate, reverse, room, shimmer-leaning per user reports) round out the ambient toolkit.
  • Random button randomizes most knobs (not Mix/Volume) — genuinely useful as a "get unstuck" tool during both practice and live performance, not just a novelty.

Part 3: Integration with your Eurorack rig (Sessions 7–9)

Session 7Evil Pet as an FX return

  • Send a generative patch (e.g. Turing Machine Mk II → Mimetic Digitalis-processed voice, or Bloom) out to the Evil Pet, granularize it, and return it to a mixer channel in parallel with the dry signal.
  • Compare this against your existing granular modules (uClouds) side by side — useful for building intuition on where Evil Pet's character differs (denser UI, built-in FX chain, feedback path) from your in-rack granular tools.

Session 8MIDI/MPE from the rack

  • If you have a MIDI-capable sequencer/keyboard controller in your chain, feed the Evil Pet 8-voice polyphonic MPE for chord-based granular pads — distinct from your CV-sequenced modular voices.
  • Layer the Evil Pet's onboard digital oscillator (sine/tri/saw/square) under processed grains for a fuller polyphonic texture.

Session 9Building a performance patch

  • Combine: one modular voice as grain source → Evil Pet Feedback for sustain → Filter sweep as the "performance knob" → Random button as a controlled wildcard → return to mixer alongside a second, untouched modular voice for contrast.
  • Rehearse muting/bypassing cleanly — Feedback patches can run away, so always know your fastest path back to silence.

Performance Tips (quick-reference, not sequential)

  • FM radio as a live sampler: unpredictable, characterful source material — great for textural surprises in an ambient set, but be mindful of copyright if recording sets for release.
  • Feedback = drone engine: your fastest path to a sustained ambient pad with zero external source material.
  • Grains knob as a rhythm control: turning past 12 o'clock for density, or left for probabilistic sparse triggering, is a fast way to move between "wash" and "glitch" live.
  • Use the LED brightness trick: shift-function knobs (Position/Spread, Size/Cloud) show the alternate value via LED brightness — glance at this instead of guessing during a set.
  • Known hardware quirks to watch for: a few users report the Position knob losing calibration (try switching the knob response mode between SNAP/JUMP if this happens) and occasional crackle independent of gain staging — worth soundchecking before a live set rather than discovering it on stage.
  • Keep a "reset" preset: given how easy it is to wander via Random and Feedback, save one baseline preset you can always jump back to mid-set.
